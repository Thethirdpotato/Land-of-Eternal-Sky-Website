CLIMATE

Mongolia experiences a continental climate characterized by vast temperature variations and distinct seasons. Winters are harsh and prolonged, with temperatures plummeting well below freezing, often reaching extremes of -40 degrees Celsius (-40 degrees Fahrenheit) in some areas. The summer months, in contrast, bring warmer temperatures, reaching highs of 30 degrees Celsius (86 degrees Fahrenheit) or more. Despite the arid conditions prevalent in much of the country, Mongolia is not devoid of precipitation. The sporadic rainfall mainly occurs during the summer, sustaining the nomadic herding lifestyle by providing crucial water sources for livestock and vegetation. The Gobi Desert, located in the southern part of Mongolia, is known for its extreme temperatures and limited rainfall, contributing to the country's overall arid climate.

FUANA

Mongolia's fauna thrives in diverse landscapes. The steppes are home to the Bactrian camel, Przewalski's horse, and Mongolian gazelle. Mountainous regions host species like the snow leopard, ibex, and argali sheep. The Gobi Desert is inhabited by the Gobi bear, wild donkey, and various bird species. Conservation efforts aim to protect this rich biodiversity, ensuring the survival of these unique and often endangered species.

VEGETATION

Mongolia's vegetation adapts to its challenging climate, with the steppes featuring a mix of grasses and shrubs supporting nomadic herding. In arid regions like the Gobi Desert, hardy plants such as saxaul and resilient grasses thrive. Mountainous areas showcase alpine meadows and deciduous forests in river valleys. This diverse flora serves vital ecological functions, preventing soil erosion and providing habitats for wildlife. Conservation initiatives work to safeguard Mongolia's plant life and the ecosystems it sustains, acknowledging their significance in preserving the country's unique environmental balance.
